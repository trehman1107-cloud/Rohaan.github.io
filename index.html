<p>Ebola Outbreak Analysis (2014-2016) Author: Rohaan Ur Rehman Date: 10 July 2026 Environment: Python 3 (Jupyter Notebook) Libraries: Pandas, Matplotlib, Seaborn</p> <p>The Ebola virus disease (EVD), formerly known as Ebola hemorrhagic fever, is a severe and often fatal illness in humans caused by the Ebolavirus. First discovered in 1976 near the Ebola River in the Democratic Republic of Congo, the virus is transmitted to humans from wild animals and spreads through human-to-human transmission via direct contact with bodily fluids.</p> <p>While previous outbreaks were largely contained to rural Central Africa, the 2014–2016 outbreak in West Africa was unprecedented in its scale and geographic reach. Originating in Guinea in late 2013, the virus rapidly spread across porous borders into Liberia and Sierra Leone. By the time the outbreak was declared over, it had resulted in over 28,000 reported cases and more than 11,000 deaths, making it the deadliest EVD outbreak in history. The crisis exposed critical vulnerabilities in global health infrastructure and highlighted the necessity of real-time epidemiological data tracking.</p> <p>Project Goals The primary objective of this project is to perform a comprehensive exploratory data analysis (EDA) on the 2014–2016 Ebola outbreak dataset. By cleaning, structuring, and visualizing this epidemiological data, this project aims to transform raw records into actionable insights.</p> <p>Specific goals include:</p> <p>Data Wrangling: Clean and preprocess two distinct CSV files containing cumulative case counts and detailed database-format indicators, handling missing values and formatting dates for time-series analysis.</p> <p>Macro-Analysis: Identify the most severely affected countries by calculating total cumulative cases and deaths to understand the geographic distribution of the outbreak.</p> <p>Temporal Tracking: Plot the progression of the outbreak over time to visualize the infection curve, identify peak transmission periods, and observe the eventual decline of cases.</p> <p>Case Classification: Utilize the detailed database file to break down total cases into "confirmed," "probable," and "suspected" categories, demonstrating how diagnostic capabilities evolved during the crisis.</p> <p>Severity Assessment: Calculate and compare the Case Fatality Rates (CFR) across different regions to assess the disease's mortality impact.</p>
<p>Importing Required Libraries For this analysis, we will use:</p> <p>Pandas for data manipulation and wrangling. Matplotlib & Seaborn for data visualization to uncover trends. Datetime for handling time-series data.</p>

# --- Cleaning Dataset 1 ---
# Rename columns for easier coding
df_cases.rename(columns={
    'Cumulative no. of confirmed, probable and suspected cases': 'Total_Cases',
    'Cumulative no. of confirmed, probable and suspected deaths': 'Total_Deaths'
}, inplace=True)

# Convert Date to datetime
df_cases['Date'] = pd.to_datetime(df_cases['Date'])

# Convert Cases and Deaths to numeric.

df_cases['Total_Cases'] = pd.to_numeric(df_cases['Total_Cases'], errors='coerce').fillna(0)
df_cases['Total_Deaths'] = pd.to_numeric(df_cases['Total_Deaths'], errors='coerce').fillna(0)

# --- Cleaning Dataset 2 ---

  1. Project Conclusions & Insights Based on the analysis of the 2014-2016 Ebola outbreak data, we can conclude the following:
  2. Devastating Regional Impact: The outbreak was overwhelmingly concentrated in three West African nations: Sierra Leone, Liberia, and Guinea. Together, these three nations accounted for over 95% of all global cases.
  3. High Mortality Rate: Ebola proved to be an extremely fatal disease. In the epicenter countries, mortality rates hovered around 30-40%, with some countries like Mali experiencing a 75% fatality rate among its few cases.
  4. Timeline: The outbreak began rapidly escalating in late 2014, peaked in late 2015, and began to plateau as international intervention and containment protocols took effect.
  5. Case Classification: In Sierra Leone (the most affected country), a large portion of early cases were merely "suspected" or "probable."
